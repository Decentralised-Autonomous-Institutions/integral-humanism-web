The Author

The man who asked whither Bharat.

Pandit Deendayal Upadhyaya was born on 25 September 1916 in Nagla Chandrabhan, a village near Mathura in Uttar Pradesh. He stood first class, first in the Ajmer Board matriculation, winning two gold medals — and won two more at Intermediate, before taking his B.A. in mathematics in first class.

He joined the RSS in 1937 as one of its earliest swayamsevaks, and the Bharatiya Jana Sangh in 1952 as its General Secretary — a post he held for fifteen years, building the party after the death of Dr. Syama Prasad Mookerjee, until he became its President in 1967.

An editor of Panchjanya and Swadesh, he wrote the Hindi drama Chandragupta Maurya, a biography of Shankaracharya, and translated the Marathi biography of Dr. Hedgewar. In April 1965 he condensed a lifetime of thought into four evening lectures in Bombay — Integral Humanism — a philosophy that begins not with systems, but with the self.

His death in tragic circumstances on 11 February 1968 was, in the words of the book's own foreword, an irreplaceable loss to the country.

The Core Philosophy

One self, widening.

Integral Humanism refuses to cut the human being into pieces — a body for the economist, a mind for the psychologist, a vote for the politician. It begins with the whole person, and lets the circle widen.

“Body, mind, intelligence and the soul — these four make up an individual. But these are integrated. We cannot think of each part separately.”
Chapter 2 · Bombay · 23 April 1965
01
एकात्म

The Integral Self

The person is not fragments to be managed but a single living whole — body, mind, intellect and soul, each with its own hunger, none to be starved for another. Knowing yourself means knowing all four at once.

02
विस्तार

The Widening Circle

Self-knowledge does not stop at the skin. The individual flows into family, family into society, society into nation, nation into humanity, humanity into all creation — concentric, never conflicting.

03
चिति

Chiti — the Soul of a Nation

As each person has a soul, each nation has a chiti — an innate nature and direction. Culture is its expression; institutions are healthy only when they are true to it. Borrowed ideologies cannot substitute for it.

04
धर्म

Dharma Sustains

Dharma is not religion, and Dharma Rajya is not theocracy. It is the moral law that holds a society together — the innate principles by which body, wealth and power are kept in the service of life.

05
पुरुषार्थ

The Four Aims

Dharma, artha, kama, moksha — righteousness, wealth, desire, liberation. Not a ladder to climb one rung at a time, but four aims pursued together, integrally, by the whole person.

06
मनुष्य

Man at the Centre

The economy exists for man, not man for the economy. A guarantee of work, the machine in the service of the human hand, and progress measured by the person standing at the very end of the line.
