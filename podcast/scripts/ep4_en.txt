Welcome back to Integral Humanism. This is episode four — the final lecture, delivered in Bombay on the twenty-fifth of April, nineteen sixty-five. Across three evenings, Deendayal Upadhyaya has argued that India lacks a direction; that the direction must come from an integrated view of life; and that society is a living organism whose soul is Chiti, sustained by Dharma, which stands above king, parliament, and majority alike. Tonight he brings it all down to earth: the state's actual duties — and above all, the economy. What would an economic system look like that serves the integral man?

He begins by recapping. By Bharatiya tradition, a nation is an organic living entity — it comes into existence on its own; nobody manufactures it. The nation brings forth institutions to fulfil its needs and to give concrete shape to its inner nature, and the state is one of these institutions — important, but not supreme. Where our literature describes the duties of a king, his importance is certainly recognised — perhaps, Upadhyaya suggests, to make him realise his immense responsibility, since he exercised tremendous influence on the lives and character of the people. Bhishma says in the Mahabharata, when asked whether circumstances make the king or the king makes the circumstances, that it is the king who shapes circumstances. Some read that as placing the king above all. Not true, he says. The king could not decide what constitutes Dharma. He only ensured that people lived according to it. The king was, in effect, today's executive.

And that reading has bite for the present day. The executive's job is to execute the laws properly — and when the executive lacks honesty and efficiency, the laws are simply disregarded, as, he says, we see very well around us. Why has prohibition failed? Who is responsible? When the very officials entrusted with implementing prohibition start taking monthly allowances from the bootleggers, how is the policy to succeed? That is the meaning of Bhishma's statement — executive responsibility, not royal supremacy. And the clinching proof from tradition: the tyrant king Venu was removed by the rishis, and Prithu enthroned in his place — and no one in history ever condemned that act; everyone hailed it. Once the supremacy of Dharma is accepted, the rishis derive from Dharma the right to remove a king who defaults. If a king does not act according to Dharma, it becomes the duty of everyone to remove him. Contrast the West: there a king was removed only by another king — or the people rejected kingship altogether — because in principle the king was God's representative and could never be removed.

In our socio-political setup, then, king and state were never supreme — and around them stood other institutions, organised both by locality and by occupation, to regulate and carry on social life. Panchayats and Janapada sabhas; associations of trades. The mightiest king never disturbed the panchayats. The guilds framed their own rules, and the state's function was mostly to see that those rules were observed by the people concerned. It never interfered in their affairs. The state touched only some aspects of society's life.

With that frame set, he turns to economics — the heart of this final lecture. What should the nature of our economic structure be? His starting principle: we must have an economic system that helps develop our humane qualities, our civilisation, and enables us to attain a still higher level of all-round perfection — a system that does not overwhelm our humanity, that does not make us slaves of its own grinding wheels. In our conception, man attains God-like perfection as the result of development. So — what structure serves that goal?

An economy must, first, produce all the basic things essential for people's maintenance and development, and for the protection and development of the nation. Fine. But once basic needs are met, should production simply go on multiplying? Here Upadhyaya turns his eye on the modern West, and what he sees is remarkable — remember, this is nineteen sixty-five, decades before anyone said "consumerism" casually. Western societies, he observes, consider it essential and even desirable to go on continuously and systematically increasing the desires and needs of man. There is no upper limit. Normally, desire precedes production — you make what people want. But now the position is reversed: people are induced to desire and use the things that have already been produced. Instead of producing to meet demand, the search is for markets for goods already made — and if demand does not exist, systematic efforts are made to create it. Earlier, production followed demand; now demand follows production. His examples are homely. Tea — did we want tea? Tea was produced, and we were taught to develop a taste for it; now it is the common man's drink, part of our life. Vegetable ghee — did anyone ever want it? It was made first, and then we were taught to use it. And the system has a whip behind it: if whatever is manufactured is not consumed, there is depression. He reminds his listeners of the great depression of nineteen thirty to thirty-two — abundance of goods, no demand, factories closed, bankruptcy and unemployment everywhere. So consumption becomes compulsory.

And then the story that steals the lecture. The editor of the Organiser weekly had visited America and brought back this gem. There is a factory that makes potato peelers — the little device for peeling potatoes. Production outstripped demand. The management called a meeting of all its salesmen to find some way to make people buy more potato peelers. And among the suggestions adopted was this: make the colour of the handle similar to the colour of a potato peel — so that along with the peelings, the peeler itself gets thrown into the garbage by mistake. Then the customer buys another one. That, plus more attractive packaging. Upadhyaya's verdict: this economic structure is not merely consumption-oriented, it is clearly leading to destruction. Throw away the old one, buy a new one! Rather than satisfying people's needs, creating fresh demand has become the aim of modern economics.

And even if you set aside the limits of natural resources, there is the question of balance in nature. Nature's parts hold each other up — like three sticks standing in mutual support: remove one, and the other two fall. The present system of production is fast disturbing that equilibrium — new products to satisfy ever-increasing desires on one side, new problems threatening the very existence of humanity on the other. His principle: use only that portion of natural resources which nature can easily recoup. When you take fruit, the tree is not injured — it may even benefit. But in the drive for bigger harvests, chemical fertilisers are used that render the land infertile within years — lakhs of acres lie barren in America from this. How long can this dance of destruction go on? Industry maintains a depreciation fund to replace machines as they wear out — then how can we neglect the depreciation fund of nature? The object of our economic system should be not extravagant use of resources, but well-regulated use. The physical things needed for a purposeful, happy, progressive life must certainly be obtained — the Almighty has provided as much. But a blind rat-race of production and consumption, as if man were created for the sole purpose of consuming — no. The engine needs coal, but the engine does not exist to consume coal; the whole art is maximum energy from minimum coal. Likewise, keeping the aim of human life in view, we should see how man reaches his goal with maximum speed on minimum fuel. Such a system will not thrive on the exploitation of nature, but will sustain nature and be nourished by it in turn. His phrase: milking, rather than exploitation. Draw the overflow of nature to sustain our lives.

If that human angle inspires the economy, our thinking transforms. In western economics — capitalist or socialist — value holds the central position; all theories centre on value. Useful for economists, perhaps, but social philosophies based entirely on value are incomplete, inhuman, and to some extent unethical. Take the common slogan: one must earn one's bread. Communists say it; capitalists don't fundamentally disagree — they differ only on who earns, and how much. Capitalists count capital and enterprise as the important components of production, and so claim the major share of profits as their due; communists count labour alone, and concede the major share to the workers. Neither is correct, says Upadhyaya. Our slogan should be: the one who earns will feed — and every person will have enough to eat. The right to food is a birthright. The ability to earn is a result of education and training. In a society, even those who do not earn must eat — the children and the old, the diseased and the invalid, all must be cared for. Society generally does fulfil this; the social and cultural progress of mankind lies in the readiness to fulfil it. Economics as a science does not account for this responsibility — but a man works not merely for his own bread; he works to shoulder this responsibility too. Otherwise, whoever had eaten his fill would stop working.

So: any economic system must provide the minimum basic necessities of human life to everyone. Food, clothing and shelter, broadly speaking. Beyond these, society must enable the individual to carry out his obligations by properly educating him; and if he falls prey to disease, society must arrange his treatment and maintenance. And then the standard by which he judges governments: if a government provides these minimum requirements, then only is it a rule of Dharma — otherwise it is a rule of Adharma. He cites Kalidasa's description of King Dilip in the Raghuvansha: being responsible for the maintenance, protection and education of his subjects, he was their true father — others were merely instrumental in giving them birth. And the king Bharat, after whom the country is named, is described the same way: by maintaining and protecting his subjects, he was called Bharat. This is his country — Bharat. If, in this country, maintenance and protection are not guaranteed, the name Bharat is meaningless.

On education, Upadhyaya is radical and completely matter-of-fact. To educate a child is in the interest of society itself. By birth, a child is an animal; he becomes a responsible member of society only by education and culture. To charge fees for something that is in society's own interest is rather odd. If children go uneducated because their parents cannot pay — will society endure that situation for long? We do not charge a fee from the tree for sowing the seed and watering it — we invest, knowing that when the tree grows we shall reap fruit. Education is exactly such an investment. An educated individual will serve society — and it will be no surprise if people grow indifferent to a society that leaves them to fend for themselves. He reminds us that before nineteen forty-seven, in all the princely states of India, education was free — the highest education, free. In the gurukulas, even food and lodging were free; the student begged bhiksha from society, and no householder would refuse a student. Society bore the burden of education. And medicine the same: it is rather surprising that medical treatment must be paid for — it too should be free, as it was in this country in the past. He can't resist a jab at commercialisation reaching even the gods: nowadays one pays even to enter a temple — at Tirupati, a quarter-anna ticket to see Balaji, except at noon for one hour of Dharma Darshan — as if, he says drily, at all other times it is Adharma Darshan.

But if everyone is guaranteed a minimum — where do the resources come from? From our own efforts. And that leads to the other half of the bargain: where a right to a guaranteed minimum is recognised, any individual who does not share in the effort to produce is a burden on society. And any system that obstructs people's productive activity is self-destructive. But work is not only about producing resources. Even if a man's needs are met while he does no work, his personality will not develop fully — his progress will be distorted and lopsided. Man has a stomach, but he also has hands. If his hands have no work, food alone will not make him happy. Just as a barren woman experiences an emptiness in life, so does a man without work. Therefore: the guarantee of work to every able-bodied member of society should be the aim of our economic system. And look at the strange situation we actually have: on one side a ten-year-old child and a seventy-year-old man are toiling; on the other, a youth of twenty-five is driven to suicide for want of work. This mismanagement must be removed.

Hands alone have limited capacity; they need the assistance of capital — machines. Labour and capital stand to each other as man and nature; the world is the creation of both, and neither can be neglected. How is capital formed? By saving a part of production from immediate consumption and using it for further production — capital comes only from restraint on consumption. This is what Marx called surplus value. In capitalism, the industrialist forms capital out of that surplus; in socialism, the state does. In both, the entire product is not distributed to the workers — and when production runs through centralised large-scale industry, the worker's sacrifice in creating that capital gets no recognition. The advantage of decentralisation is precisely that the worker has a sense of direct participation in the management of the surplus — of the capital he helped create.

Now the machine itself. The machine is the most common form of capital, and it was created to reduce the physical burden of labour and increase the worker's productivity. The machine is the worker's assistant — not his competitor. But where human labour is treated as a commodity to be bought with money, the machine becomes the human being's competitor — displacing him, subjecting him to privation — and the very purpose of creating the machine is defeated. Don't blame the machine, he says; blame the economic and social system that cannot distinguish between the object and the instrument. So we must consider the limits of the machine's usefulness and decide its field of application. And a specific warning for India: to import machinery from western countries — where shortage of manpower was the guiding factor in machine design — would be a serious mistake. The merits of a machine are not independent of time and place. Machines are a product of modern science, not its representatives; scientific knowledge belongs to no country, but its application must fit each country's conditions. Our machines must be tailored to our economic needs, and must at least not conflict with our socio-political and cultural objectives. We shall have to develop, in his phrase, a Bharatiya technology.

He borrows a framework from Professor Visvesvaraya: in designing a system of production, seven M's must be considered — man, material, money, management, motive power, market, and machine. The skills of the workers who need work; the availability and quality of raw materials; how much capital there is, how it can grow, how best used, how much fixed and how much liquid; what motive power the country can economically obtain — wind, water, steam, oil, gas, electricity, atomic power; the managerial skill to coordinate — without it, a dozen workers all stay unemployed; the market — production of a commodity cannot be justified without considering the market it commands; and only then, the machine, designed to suit all the rest. Instead, he observes, we nowadays install the machine first and try to coordinate the other factors afterwards — no country progressed that way; if the pioneers had done that, new machines would never have been invented at all. We import machines, and so we remain without knowledge.

None of the seven factors is fixed; each keeps changing, and planners must direct the change toward progress, less hardship, less waste. Take our workers' low productivity: machines can raise it, and should. But if a machine needs only a few men where many need work, the rest are thrown out of employment; and if an imported machine costs so much that its extra production cannot pay for it, it does not suit us. An idle part of a factory's installed capacity is a losing proposition — but idle people are worse: a machine ties up only the capital sunk in it, while the unemployed must go on being fed, a continuous drain, consuming at double speed. Therefore, instead of the usual slogan "every worker must get food," we must make the basis of our economy: everyone who eats must get work. Yes, the charkha will have to yield to machines — but not necessarily to automatic machines everywhere. Full employment first; then fit the other six factors around it.

Then he lifts the argument to its real subject: man's place in the economy. The economic theories of the past few centuries, and the societies built on them, have thoroughly devalued the human being — his personality has become irrelevant to the economic setup. Capitalism recognises only the "economic man", whose every decision is a calculation of gain and loss in material wealth — for whom five rupees are always more than four. Human labour is a commodity, bought and sold in the market. Free enterprise tolerates no restraint except the brake of competition; in the race, nobody stops to lend the weak a hand — elimination of the weak is considered just and natural: he is an uneconomic, marginal unit, unfit to exist. And by eliminating such marginal units, economic power accumulates in a few hands — which capitalism calls normal. But once monopoly is established, even the check of competition ceases: prices are fixed arbitrarily, quality deteriorates. As for the consumer — the capitalist attends not to needs but to purchasing power: the wants of the wealthy and well-fed are served while the basic necessities of the poor grow scarce. Production centralises, markets standardise, individual preference is ignored — human beings are allotted numbers like books in a library. The system that boasts of giving highest importance to the individual has, ironically, destroyed all individuality. Capitalism, he concludes, is incapable of helping the development of an integral human being.

And socialism? Socialism arose as a reaction to capitalism — but it too failed to establish the importance of the human being. The socialists contented themselves with transferring the ownership of capital to the state — and the state is an even more impersonal institution, run by rigid rules with no place for individual discretion; and wherever discretion is allowed, the slightest laxity in duty produces corruption and favouritism. Capitalism thought only of the economic man but at least left him free elsewhere; socialism went further and thought only of an abstract man, leaving no scope for personality, for diverse tastes and abilities. The needs and preferences of individuals have as much importance in the socialist system, he says, as in a prison manual. There is no individual freedom. Private property disappears — and with it, its problems — but also its incentives for production, conservation, and economy, for which no alternative was ever arranged. The state becomes the supreme and sole authority; the citizen is a cog in a giant wheel, with nothing to inspire him to his role. He cites Milovan Djilas — the Yugoslav dissident — the old class of exploiters has been eliminated, and a new class of bureaucratic exploiters has taken its place. And Marx's determinism — capitalism inevitably destroying itself, communism inevitably following — may foster faith in victory, but it destroys the urge for reform and the dynamism of man: he is no longer the creator of a new order, merely incidental to a predetermined process, and so the organiser of workers cares little for their welfare, using them as tools for the revolution. Even the dialectic itself is halted once the socialist state is supreme: in the name of crushing counter-revolutionaries the state grows more and more totalitarian, and the day it is to wither away remains a dream. By the Marxist's own standard — that obstructing the process of thesis and antithesis is reactionary — Marx stands falsified by his own state.

Both systems, then — capitalist and communist — have failed to take account of the integral man. One treats him as a selfish creature chasing money under the law of the jungle; the other as a feeble, lifeless cog regulated by rigid rules, incapable of good unless directed. Both imply centralisation of power, economic and political. Both, therefore, end in the dehumanisation of man. And here Upadhyaya delivers the sentence the whole series has been building toward: Man, the highest creation of God, is losing his own identity. We must re-establish him in his rightful position, bring him the realisation of his greatness, reawaken his abilities, and encourage him to exert himself for attaining the divine heights of his latent personality — and this is possible only through a decentralised economy. We want neither capitalism nor socialism. We aim at the progress and happiness of Man — the Integral Man. The protagonists of the two systems fight with man on the stage; neither understands him, neither cares for his interests.

So what, positively, are the objectives of our economy? He lists them. One: an assurance of a minimum standard of living to every individual, and preparedness for the defence of the nation. Two: further increase above this minimum, so that individual and nation acquire the means to contribute to world progress on the basis of their own Chiti. Three: meaningful employment to every able-bodied citizen — the means by which the first two are realised — avoiding waste and extravagance in the use of natural resources. Four: machines suited to Bharatiya conditions — a Bharatiya technology — designed with the seven M's in view. Five: the system must help, and never disregard, the human being — protecting the cultural and other values of life; a requirement that cannot be violated except at great peril. Six: the ownership of industries — state, private, or any other form — decided pragmatically, case by case. And the two words that summarise the whole policy for present circumstances: Swadeshi and decentralisation. For years, he says, centralisation and monopolisation have been the order of the day — planners imprisoned by the belief that only large-scale centralised industry is economic. And Swadeshi is ridiculed as old-fashioned and reactionary while we proudly use foreign articles and grow dependent on foreign aid for everything — thinking, management, capital, methods, technology, down to the very standards and forms of our consumption. This, he says, is not the road to progress. We shall forget our individuality and become virtual slaves once again. The positive content of Swadeshi must be the cornerstone of our economic reconstruction.

He admits, for want of time, that many structural details remain untouched. But one thing is clear: many old institutions will yield to new ones. Those with vested interests in the old will suffer; so will those averse to all change. But disease must be treated with medicine; strength comes only from exercise and hard work. Discard the status-quo mentality — and equally, let reconstruction not be clouded by prejudice against everything inherited from the past, nor cling to institutions that have outlived their utility.

And then the summing up of all four evenings. On the basis of Integral Humanism, we shall be able to reconcile nationalism, democracy, socialism and world peace with the traditional values of Bharatiya culture — think of all these ideals in an integrated form, remove their mutual conflicts, and make them complement one another — so that Man may regain his lost status and attain the aims of his life. But — and this is the activist speaking — the members of the Jana Sangh are not mere philosophers or academicians; the determination is to make the nation strong, happy and prosperous through practical programmes on this foundation. We have taken due note of our ancient culture — but we are not archaeologists, and we have no intention of becoming custodians of a vast archaeological museum. The goal is not merely to protect the culture but to revitalise it — make it dynamic and in tune with the times. Some traditions must end: where reforms help the growth of values and of national unity, we shall make them; where traditions obstruct, we shall remove them. One need not mourn the limitations of the human body — but if a part of the body develops a cancerous growth, the operation must be performed; and there is no need to amputate healthy limbs. If society is gripped today by evils like untouchability — which lead men to treat other human beings as lower than themselves and thereby threaten national unity — such evils shall have to end.

And we shall need new institutions — institutions that kindle the spirit of action, that replace self-centredness and selfishness with a desire to serve the nation, that produce not merely sympathy toward our brethren but affection and a sense of oneness with them. Such institutions can truly reflect our Chiti. And here, in the closing minutes, he introduces the last concept of the series: Virat. Chiti is the nation's soul; the strength and energy that activate the nation are called Virat — channelled by Chiti. Virat's place in the life of a nation is like that of Prana, the life-breath, in the body: as Prana infuses strength into the organs, refreshes the intellect, and keeps body and soul together — so, in a nation, only with a strong Virat can democracy succeed and government be effective. When Virat is awake, diversity — of language, of occupation — does not lead to conflict; people cooperate like the limbs of one body, like members of a family.

So the task: to awaken our nation's Virat. Let us go forward, he says, with pride in our heritage, a realistic assessment of the present, and great ambition for the future. We wish neither to make this country a shadow of some distant past, nor an imitation of Russia or America. With the support of universal knowledge and of our heritage, we shall create a Bharat that will excel all its past glories — one that enables every citizen in its fold to steadily develop his manifold latent possibilities, and, through a sense of unity with the entire creation, to achieve a state even higher than that of the complete human being — to become Narayan from Nar. Man into the divine. This, he says, is the eternal, divine form of our culture — this is our message to a humanity standing at the crossroads. May God give us strength to succeed in this task.

And with the cry of Bharat Mata ki Jai, on the evening of the twenty-fifth of April, nineteen sixty-five, the four lectures end.

That completes Integral Humanism — all four lectures. Not a museum piece, but an argument: that the human being is one whole; that a nation is a living self; that Dharma stands above every throne, every parliament, and every majority; and that an economy is civilised only when the machine serves the man. Whatever you make of it, you have now heard the whole of it. Thank you for listening.
