Welcome back to Integral Humanism. Episode three — the third lecture, delivered in Bombay on the twenty-fourth of April, nineteen sixty-five. The first evening diagnosed a nation without direction. The second built the picture of the integral human being — body, mind, intellect and soul. Tonight the question widens: man does not exist merely as an individual. The "I" is inseparably bound to the "We". So what exactly is society? Where does a nation come from? And who, in the end, is sovereign? This is the longest and perhaps the deepest of the four lectures.

Upadhyaya starts with the question philosophers have always asked: how did society come into being? The dominant western answer — the one their whole socio-political structure is built on — is the social contract theory: society is a group of individuals brought into being by an agreement among those individuals. The individual comes first. Western thinkers differ only on the follow-up questions: if individuals produced society, where does residual power rest — with society or with the individual? Can the individual change society at will? Can society impose regulations and demand allegiance? From these questions, whole schools of thought — and a standing controversy of individual versus society, with some choosing the individual supreme, others the society, and conflict built into the very frame.

Upadhyaya's response is direct: the view that individuals brought society into being is fundamentally incorrect. Yes, society is composed of individuals. But it is not made by them, and it does not arise from their mere coming together. In our view, he says, society is self-born. Like an individual, society comes into existence organically. People do not produce society. It is not a club, not a joint stock company, not a registered cooperative. Society is an entity with its own self, its own life — a sovereign being like an individual — an organic entity. Society, too, has its body, its mind, its intellect, and its soul. And he notes that some western psychologists were beginning to see this — McDougall produced a branch of psychology called the group mind, accepting that a group has its own mind, its own psychology, its own ways of thinking and acting.

Group feelings, he explains, are not the arithmetic sum of individual feelings. Group strength is not the sum of individual strengths. The intellect, emotions, and energies of a group differ fundamentally from the individual's. That is why a physical weakling can turn out to be a heroic member of society. A man may swallow a personal insult — but abuse his society, and the same mild man loses his temper. A person of high character in private life can be unscrupulous as a member of society — and the reverse.

He illustrates with a conversation he'd heard of, between Vinoba Bhave and Shri Guruji, the chief of the R.S.S. The question arose: where do the modes of thinking of Hindus and Muslims differ? Guruji said — there are good and bad people in every society; honest men among Hindus and among Muslims, rascals among both; no society has a monopoly on goodness. But, he observed, when Hindus come together in a group, even those who are rascals in individual life begin to think of good things. And when Muslims come together, they propose and approve things which individually they would never think of. Vinoba, the account goes, admitted there was truth in the observation but had no explanation for it. Upadhyaya's point in telling it is analytical, not polemical: individual thinking and group thinking are simply different things — a thousand good men gathered together will not necessarily think good thoughts. Today's average student is milder and meeker than the student of twenty years ago — but put a score of them together and they turn undisciplined, capable of all sorts of irresponsible actions. That is mob mentality. But note — mob mentality is a passing, small-scale thing, the psychology of a crowd gathered for an hour. A society's mentality evolves over a much longer time.

Then a common thesis: when people live together long enough, tradition and association make them think alike — and that is what a nation is. Upadhyaya tests that idea against history and finds it false. Some ancient nations disappeared. Greece. Egypt. Babylon, Syria. Was there ever a moment when their citizens stopped living together? Never. The Greece that produced Alexander and Herodotus, Ulysses and Aristotle, Socrates and Plato — present-day Greece is inhabited by the same hereditary stock; father-to-son continuity was never interrupted; you could trace today's Greeks back two hundred and fifty, five hundred generations. And yet the old Greek nation is non-existent. Egypt likewise. New nations stand in those places. So nations do not arise from mere cohabitation. And the counter-case: the Jews. Scattered for centuries among other peoples, living far and wide — and yet never annihilated as a people. The source of nationhood is not residence on a piece of land.

So what is a nation? Here is his definition. When a group of people lives with a goal, an ideal, a mission — and looks upon a particular piece of land as its motherland — that group constitutes a nation. Both elements are necessary. If either is missing — the ideal, or the motherland — there is no nation.

And like the individual, the nation has a soul. The body has a self, and when the self's relation with the body is severed, we say the person has died. Similarly there is a fundamental idea, an ideal, at the core of a nation — its soul. He explains identity through a story that philosophers would enjoy. A barber, shaving a customer, boasts that his razor is sixty years old — his father had used the same razor. The customer is surprised: the handle looks shiny and new. "Oh, the handle was replaced six months ago." And how old is the steel? "Three years." The handle replaced, the steel replaced — but the razor remains sixty years old! Its identity is intact. That is the law of identity — and a nation's identity persists the same way, through all change of generations and forms, because something at its core continues. In the Principles and Policies adopted by the Jana Sangh, this core has a technical name: Chiti. Following McDougall, he calls it the innate nature of a group — inborn, and not the result of historical circumstances.

He distinguishes carefully. A human being is born with a soul; personality is different — personality is the cumulative effect of all one's actions, thoughts and impressions, while the soul is untouched by that history. In the same way, national culture is continuously modified and enlarged by history and circumstance — culture includes everything the society's endeavours have come to hold as good and commendable. But culture is not Chiti. Chiti is fundamental; it is central to the nation from its very beginning, regardless of history. Chiti determines the direction of the nation's cultural advance: whatever is in accordance with Chiti is approved and added to culture; whatever is against it is discarded as perversion, as undesirable, to be avoided. Chiti is the touchstone on which every action and every attitude is tested.

How do you see Chiti at work? Through the judgments a civilisation makes. Take the Mahabharata. The Kauravas were defeated, the Pandavas won. Why do we hold up the conduct of the Pandavas as Dharma? Why was that war never regarded as just a battle for a kingdom? The praise for Yudhishthira and the dishonour heaped on Duryodhana have no political cause. Krishna killed his own uncle Kansa, the established king of the time — and instead of branding it a revolt, we call Krishna an avatar of God and Kansa an asura. Rama's invasion of Lanka was assisted by Vibhishana — Ravana's own brother. He betrayed his brother and his king, exactly as Jaichand did centuries later. By all ordinary standards he should be branded a quisling. Yet no one calls Vibhishana a quisling — he is praised, and it is Ravana's actions that stand condemned. Why? Because the standard by which these actions were judged was not political. It was Chiti. On the strength of Chiti a nation rises, strong and virile — and Chiti is what shines through the actions of every great man of a nation.

The individual, then, carries more than his own self: he is an instrument through which the nation's soul expresses itself. He also mans the institutions created for the national goal — and beyond the nation, he represents mankind. A person is a multitude of aspects — and they are not conflicting; there is cooperation, unity, and harmony among them. A system built on recognising this mutual complementarity — a system whose laws remove disharmony and enhance cooperation — alone can bring peace and happiness.

Now, institutions. By Darwin's theory, living beings develop organs as circumstances require; our shastras said it slightly differently — the soul, using the strength of Prana, builds the organs life needs. In the same way the nation brings forth institutions — instruments to achieve national goals, like departments in a factory. Family, caste, guilds — today's trade unions — property, marriage: all institutions. There was a time before marriage existed; some rishi established the practice — created the institution. Gurukul and Rishikul, likewise. And — this is the crucial one — the state is also an institution. The nation creates it. A lot of the trouble in the West, Upadhyaya says, comes from confusing the state with the nation — treating them as synonymous. They are not the same. And here is the delicious reversal: in our country it is the state that arose by social contract, while society is self-born. The Mahabharata describes that in Krita-yuga there was no state and no king — society was sustained by Dharma alone, mutually practised. Then decline set in; greed and anger dominated; might became right. The rishis, perturbed, went to Brahma, who gave them a treatise on law and the functions of the state, and asked Manu to become the first king. And Manu refused! A king must punish people, put them in jail — Manu was not prepared to commit such sins. Brahma reassured him: actions taken as king, aimed at securing conditions in which society can live peacefully and according to Dharma, will not be sin — that is your duty, your Dharma. And more: you will receive a share of the Dharma your subjects earn by righteous conduct. Upadhyaya adds his own gloss with a smile — presumably a share of the sins as well; it would hardly be proper for the king to share only the good things. So in Bharat the state came into existence by a contract — the contract theory applies to the state, not the nation. In the West it was exactly the opposite: society was the contract, while the king claimed divine right as God's sole representative. That, he says flatly, is wrong.

The individual, meanwhile, belongs to many institutions at once — family, community, professional association, nation, mankind, the entire universe. Truly speaking, an individual is not a single entity but a plural entity — living a variety of lives. And the essential human quality is the capacity to carry all these memberships without letting them collide — mutually sustaining, complementary, unified. The man who uses this quality well becomes happy; the man who fails, suffers. One man is son to his mother, husband to his wife, brother to his sister, father to his son — he must maintain all these relations with intelligence, understanding and tact. When he cannot — when the claims of mother and wife clash and he fumbles — there is conflict and pain. When he resolves them and fulfils all his obligations properly, his development is integrated.

From here, the social conclusion: we do not accept any permanent, inevitable conflict between the individual's many roles, or among the institutions of society. Where conflict exists, it is a sign of decadence, of perversion — not of nature or culture. The West's error is to make conflict fundamental — the individual versus the state, and on the same logic, class against class. Classes exist, yes. We had castes. But we never accepted conflict between them as fundamental. In our conception the four varnas were thought of as the limbs of the Virat-Purusha — the cosmic person: the Brahmin from the head, the Kshatriya from the arms, the Vaishya from the abdomen, the Shudra from the legs. Ask yourself: can there be conflict among the head, the arms, the stomach and the legs of the same body? If conflict were fundamental, the body could not be maintained. The limbs are complementary — more, there is complete identity of interest, identity of belonging. That was the basis. And he is honest about the present: if that living idea is not kept alive, the castes, instead of being complementary, produce conflict — and that is distortion; not an arrangement but the absence of any arrangement. That, he says, is indeed the present condition of our society.

Institutions can deteriorate like this for many reasons — above all, when the soul of the society weakens, every limb grows feeble, and a particular institution may become useless or even harmful. Their need and usefulness change with time, place and circumstance. The standards for judging them remain the same: mutual complementarity, and a sense of unity. Family, community, trade union, gram panchayat, janapada, state — all limbs of the nation, interdependent and complementary.

And the state? One institution among several. Important — but not above all others. A major cause of the modern world's problems, he says, is that practically everyone treats the state as synonymous with society, as society's sole representative — other institutions decline while all power centralises in the state. Bharat took the other view, and it saved us. Our national life continued uninterrupted even when the state fell into foreign hands. The Persian nation ended with its loss of independence. Here, the Pathans seized the throne of Delhi, then the Turks, the Mughals, the British — and our national life went on, because the state was not its centre. He recalls the children's tale of the demon whose life resided in a parrot — kill the parrot and the demon dies. Nations whose life is centred in the state die with the state. Ours did not. But he immediately marks the cost, quoting Dr. Ambedkar: our village panchayats were so strong that we neglected the throne of Delhi. We were not as alert about the state as we should have been. The state may not be the centre of national life — but it is an important limb. You can pluck a hair without harm; take skin with it and it hurts; go further, take the head, and the body is lost. The limbs matter. That is why Samarth Ramdas — a saint who could just as easily have told Shivaji to become a mendicant like himself — instead directed Shivaji to establish a kingdom. The state matters. But important is not the same as supreme.

Then what is supreme? His answer is the spine of the whole lecture: Dharma. Consider why the state exists at all. It must have an aim, an ideal — and that ideal must rank higher than the state created to serve it. A watchman is not greater than the treasure he guards. The state is brought into existence to protect the nation, and to produce and maintain the conditions in which the nation's ideals — its Chiti — can be translated into reality. The laws that help manifest and maintain the Chiti of a nation are the Dharma of that nation. Hence Dharma is supreme. Dharma is the repository of the nation's soul. If Dharma is destroyed, the nation perishes. Whoever abandons Dharma, betrays the nation.

And at once, the vital clarification — because this is where the mistranslation has done a century of damage. Dharma is not confined to temples and mosques. Worship of God is only a part of Dharma. Temples have served as an effective medium for teaching Dharma — but just as schools do not themselves constitute knowledge, temples do not constitute Dharma. A child may attend school daily and remain uneducated; a man may visit temple or mosque without a break and never know his Dharma. Religion — a creed, a sect — is not Dharma. In the West, atrocities were committed and wars fought in the name of religion, and because we translated Dharma as religion, all of that was debited to Dharma's account too. But battles of religion and battles for Dharma are different things. Dharma is a very wide concept, concerned with all aspects of life. It sustains society. It sustains the whole world. That which sustains — is Dharma.

Its fundamental principles are eternal and universal — but their implementation changes with time, place and circumstance. Everyone needs food — but what a particular person should eat, how much, when — that depends. For a typhoid patient, fasting may be the prescription. Some rules are momentary: in this meeting, the rule is that I speak and you listen — break it, and the meeting cannot be sustained; observing it is, for this hour, your Dharma. But carry that rule home and refuse to speak to your family, and someone will call a doctor! Rules must fit the entity they sustain — and they must sit within the larger framework of Dharma, the way a registered society's rules must conform to its constitution, the constitution to the Societies Registration Act, the act to the constitution of the country. And the constitution of the country itself — is it arbitrary, the mere product of an assembly's decisions? No: it too must follow the fundamental principles of nature, the basic law. A constitution exists to sustain the nation; if instead it becomes instrumental in the nation's deterioration, it is improper, and must be amended.

And amendment is not simply a matter of majority. Here Upadhyaya mounts one of the most striking arguments of the whole book — against the sovereignty of everything, including the people. Is the majority capable of doing anything and everything? Is the action of the majority always just? No. In the West the king was sovereign; when royalty fell, sovereignty passed to the people. In our country, neither kings, nor the people, nor parliament have ever had absolute sovereignty. They say of the British Parliament that it can do everything except make a woman a man and a man a woman. But can Parliament legislate that every Englishman shall walk on his head? Can it require every citizen to report to the local authority once a day? It cannot. England has no written constitution — they respect tradition; and their traditions change by one standard only: whatever obstructed England's progress was discarded, whatever helped was consolidated. We have a written constitution — but even a written constitution cannot go contrary to the traditions of this country; in so far as it does, it is not fulfilling Dharma. The constitution that sustains the nation is in tune with Dharma — and Dharma is sovereign. All other entities, institutions and authorities derive their power from Dharma and are subordinate to it.

He then applies this to the constitution we actually have. We are one nation, one society — we rightly refused special rights based on language, province, caste or religion, gave everyone a single equal citizenship, denied states the right to secede, vested the drawing of state boundaries in Parliament. All in tune with Bharat's nationhood. And yet — we made the constitution federal, rejecting in principle what we adopted in practice. In a federation, the constituent units hold original sovereignty and surrender it by agreement — the centre has no power of its own. That is contrary to the truth of Bharat; it runs against her unity and indivisibility. The first article — India, that is Bharat, shall be a union of states — as if Bihar Mata plus Banga Mata plus Punjab Mata plus Kannada Mata plus Tamil Mata add up to Bharat Mata! Ridiculous, he says. The provinces are limbs of Bharat Mata, not separate mothers. The constitution should be unitary. But — and mark this, because he is often misread here — unitary does not mean all power concentrated at the centre. The head of a family does not hold all powers even though transactions run in his name; in the body, the soul does not possess every power. A unitary state does not mean an autocratic centre or the elimination of provinces. Provinces will have executive powers; below them the janapadas; and the panchayats — which tradition never allowed anyone to dissolve — should have powers in their own right, not exist at the mercy of the states as delegated authorities. Power distributed to the lowest level, fully decentralised — and all of it centred around the unitary state. That arrangement, he says, embodies Dharma.

Dharma reaches beyond written law altogether. There are norms found in no statute book that bind more strongly than statutes. No government has passed a law that one should respect one's parents — yet people do, and those who don't are criticised. The fundamental law of human nature is the standard for judging behaviour — and that law is Dharma. The nearest English equivalent he offers is innate law — and even that, he admits, does not express the full meaning.

Since Dharma is supreme, our ideal of the state has always been Dharma Rajya — the rule of Dharma. He reaches for a marvellous piece of ritual history. In the old coronation ceremony, the king would recite three times: "There is no authority that can punish me" — the same claim western kings made, the king can do no wrong. And upon this, the purohit would strike the king on the back with a staff and say: No. You are subject to the rule of Dharma. You are not sovereign. The king would run around the sacred fire with the priest following, striking him — three rounds — so that the king was told, unambiguously and in public, that Dharma stood above him.

Can the people, then, do whatever they please — isn't that what democracy means? No, he says: even the people are not free to act contrary to Dharma. He plays with the old theological riddle: if God is omnipotent, can God act against Dharma? If He does, He is not omnipotent — because Adharma is a characteristic of weakness, not strength. A fire that stops giving heat is not a strong fire; it has ceased to be fire. Strength lies not in unrestrained behaviour but in well-regulated action. God is omnipotent precisely because He is perfectly self-regulated — He descends in human form to destroy Adharma and re-establish Dharma, not to act on whim. At the risk of being misunderstood, Upadhyaya says, one may even say Dharma is greater than God — the universe is sustained because God acts according to Dharma.

Now — and he is very careful here — Dharma Rajya does not mean a theocratic state. Let us be absolutely clear, he says. Where one sect and its prophet or guru rule supreme — where the rights belong to the followers of that sect and everyone else lives as a second-class citizen or cannot live at all — that is theocracy. The Holy Roman Empire had that basis; so did the Khilafat, in whose name Muslim kings the world over ruled; and the most recent theocratic state is Pakistan, the self-declared Islamic State, where all non-Muslims are second-class citizens. And he adds the cutting observation: apart from that difference, there is no other sign of Islam in Pakistan's administration — the Quran, the masjid, roza, Id, namaz are the same in Bharat as in Pakistan. Tying the state to a religion adds nothing to anyone's capacity to worship God; the only result is that the state slips in its duty. Dharma Rajya is the opposite: it accepts the importance of religion in the peace, happiness and progress of the individual, and therefore takes responsibility for maintaining an atmosphere in which every individual can follow the religion of his choice and live in peace. Freedom has inherent limits, of course — I have the freedom to swing my hand, but my freedom ends where your nose begins. Every religion is free to exist — and that freedom extends only as far as it does not encroach upon the religion of others; encroachment is misuse and must be ended. Dharma Rajya ensures religious freedom. It is not a theocratic state.

What of the fashionable term "secular state"? A mere imitation of western thought patterns, he says — we had no need to import it; we adopted it to contrast ourselves with Pakistan. And it breeds confusion: religion was equated with Dharma, so a secular state was taken to mean a state without Dharma — some said Dharma-nirpeksha, indifferent to Dharma. All fundamentally erroneous. A state can no more be without Dharma than fire can be without heat. Fire that loses heat is no longer fire. The state exists fundamentally to maintain Dharma — to maintain law and order. A state without Dharma is a lawless state, and where there is lawlessness, what state is there at all? The state can only be Dharma Rajya — any other definition contradicts the very reason for its existence.

In a Dharma Rajya, the state is not absolutely powerful — sovereignty rests in Dharma. He applies it to a live controversy: is Parliament sovereign, or the Supreme Court? Legislature or judiciary? A quarrel, he says, like arguing whether the left hand is more important or the right. Both are limbs of the state; each is supreme in its own sphere, with distinct functions. The legislature says "we are higher — we made the constitution and can amend it"; the judiciary says "we interpret what you make". But even if the constitution is amended by majority, it can still be against Dharma. Legislature and judiciary stand on an equal plane — Dharma is higher than both, and Dharma specifies the limits of both. Nor are the people supreme — "but the people elect!" — even the people have no right to act against Dharma. If an elected government lets people act against Dharma and does not punish, it is in reality a government of thieves. Even the general will cannot override Dharma. Imagine thieves, by some manoeuvring, gaining a majority and electing one of their own to rule — what is the duty of the minority? Clearly, to remove him.

And history supplies the proofs. When Hitler overran France, Marshal Pétain decided to surrender — and French public opinion supported the surrender. De Gaulle escaped to London and declared he did not accept it: France is independent and will remain so. He formed a government in exile and eventually liberated France. If majority rule were supreme, De Gaulle's action stands condemned — he had no right to fight. His right came from the fact that the French nation was above majority public opinion. Independence is the Dharma of every nation; to preserve it, and to strive to regain it when lost, is the duty of every citizen. In our own country, the majority had not risen against the British — only a few had. Lokmanya Tilak declared "Freedom is my birthright" — he did not establish that birthright by referendum. And so, to the current demands: a plebiscite in Kashmir, a referendum on Goa. Wrong, says Upadhyaya. National unity is our Dharma; decisions about it cannot be made by plebiscite — nature has already decided. Elections and majorities can decide who forms the government. They cannot decide the truth. Even if, of the four hundred and fifty million people of India, all but one opt for something against Dharma, it does not become truth. And if one single person stands with Dharma, that is truth — because truth resides with Dharma — and it is that one person's duty to tread the path of truth and change the people. Lincoln knew this: when the southern states, on the question of slavery, declared their intention to secede, he told them — you have no right to secede, even in a democracy. He refused compromise, refused to meet slavery halfway, declared it against the very principles at the basis of the American nation, fought a civil war over it — and did not compromise with Adharma. Like the old Hindu marriage, Upadhyaya adds, where the couple could not divorce even if both wished it — some bonds are regulated by Dharma, not by sweet will. Kashmir and Goa are such bonds.

So Dharma is not necessarily with the majority, or with the people. Dharma is eternal. And therefore the definition of democracy — government of the people, by the people, for the people — needs completing: "of" stands for independence; "by" stands for democracy; and "for" indicates Dharma. True democracy exists only where there is freedom — and Dharma. Dharma Rajya encompasses all of these.

That is the third lecture.
