Welcome back to Integral Humanism. This is episode two, covering the second lecture, delivered in Bombay on the twenty-third of April, nineteen sixty-five. If the first lecture asked "whither Bharat" and showed why the West cannot answer that question for us, this second lecture gives the answer's foundation: the integrated view of life — and at its centre, the integral human being.

Upadhyaya opens by recalling the previous evening. Seventeen years after independence, we have still to decide what direction to take to realise our dream of all-round development. And most people, he notes, aren't prepared to seriously consider the question at all. They think only of problems as they arise — an economic problem today, a social or political one tomorrow. Without knowing fundamentally where we want to go, these efforts carry no enthusiasm, give no satisfaction, and produce only a fraction of the results they should.

Among those who do advocate a definite direction, there are two camps. One says: go back to the position we were in when we lost our independence a thousand years ago, and proceed from there. The other says: discard everything that originated in Bharat, don't even think about it — western life and thought are the last word in progress, import it all. And Upadhyaya's judgment is even-handed: both lines of thought are incorrect, though both contain partial truths that shouldn't be discarded altogether.

To the revivalists, he says: whether or not going back a thousand years is desirable, it is definitely impossible. The flow of time cannot be reversed. And here he adds something important — the past cannot be disowned either. Whatever we assimilated in those thousand years, whether forced on us or accepted willingly, cannot simply be cast off now. And we were not passive all that time. We created, not a little, of our own. We attempted to reshape our life to face every new situation. So it will not do to close our eyes to a thousand years of our own history.

To the westernisers, he says: you forget that these ideologies arose in particular situations, at particular times, among particular peoples. They are not necessarily universal, and they are not free from the limitations of the culture that produced them. Many are already out of date. The principles of Marx have changed with changing times and conditions — so much so that a parrot-like repetition of Marxism for India's problems would be a reactionary attitude, not a scientific one. And he allows himself the obvious irony: it is surprising that people who claim to reform society by removing its dead traditions themselves fall prey to outdated foreign traditions.

Then a homely, clinching analogy. Every country has its own peculiar historical, social and economic situation, and its leaders prescribe remedies with that background in mind. The same basic organic activity runs in all human beings — but a drug that helps in England may not help in India. Disease depends on climate, water, dietary habits, heredity. The same drug does not cure all persons even when the external symptoms look similar. Those who apply a single panacea to all diseases are quacks, not doctors. That is why Ayurveda says: for the disease in each place, a remedy suitable to that place must be found. So it is neither possible nor wise to transplant foreign isms into our country in their original form, wholesale.

But immediately, the balance swings back — because Upadhyaya is no isolationist. Not all thought that springs up elsewhere is local in space and time. Human responses in one place and time can have use for human beings elsewhere. To ignore the developments of other societies, past or present, is certainly unwise. Whatever truth they contain must be noted and accepted; the rest scrupulously avoided; and even their wisdom must be adapted to our circumstances. His summary rule: absorb the knowledge and gains of all humanity as far as eternal principles and truths are concerned. Those that originated among us must be clarified and adapted to changed times; those we take from others must be adapted to our conditions.

Now he returns to the West's three ideals — nationalism, democracy, socialism, plus the attempts at world unity, the League of Nations, and after the second war, the United Nations. All of these, in practice, have proved incomplete and mutually opposed. Nationalism led to conflict between nations, and to global war. Yet if the status quo is treated as world peace, the aspirations of small nations for independence would never be fulfilled — so world unity and nationalism conflict. Democracy grants individual liberty — which the capitalist system then uses for exploitation and monopoly. Socialism came in to end exploitation — and eliminated the freedom and dignity of the individual. His verdict is stark: mankind stands confused, unable to decide the correct path. The West itself is groping; it cannot say with confidence, "this alone is the right path." Therefore, simply to follow the West would be a case of one blind man being led by another.

And so, he says, our attention is claimed by Bharatiya culture. Is it possible that our culture can point the direction — not just for us, but for the world? From the national standpoint we must consider our culture because it is our very nature — independence is meaningful only as an instrument for the expression of one's culture; otherwise the freedom movement reduces to a scramble of selfish, power-seeking persons. And if, with our culture's help, we can also reconcile the conflicting ideals of western political thought — that is a service to mankind, because those ideals do represent real human aspirations and should not be ignored.

So what is the first principle of Bharatiya culture? This: it looks upon life as an integrated whole. It has an integrated viewpoint. Thinking in parts may be proper for a specialist, but it fails in practice — and the West's confusion, he argues, comes precisely from thinking of life in sections and then trying to stitch them together with patchwork. Yes, there is diversity and plurality in life. But we have always attempted to discover the unity behind it. And this attempt, he insists, is thoroughly scientific. What does a scientist do? He seeks order in apparent disorder. Chemists found that a few elements make up the entire physical world. Physicists went further and showed the elements are only energy. Today we know the entire universe is a form of energy. Philosophers are basically scientists too. Western philosophers reached the principle of duality. Hegel gave thesis, antithesis, synthesis; Marx built his analysis of history and economics on Hegel's principle; Darwin made survival of the fittest the sole basis of life. But in this country, we saw the basic unity of all life. Even our dualists held nature and spirit to be complementary, not conflicting. The diversity of life is an expression of internal unity. Think of a seed: from one seed come the roots, the trunk, the branches, the leaves, the flowers, the fruit — different forms, different colours, even different properties — yet we recognise their unity through the one seed.

Unity in diversity, and the expression of unity in many forms — this, he says, is the central thought of Bharatiya culture. And it has a sharp consequence: if this truth is accepted wholeheartedly, there remains no cause for conflict between different powers and interests. Conflict is not a sign of culture or of nature — it is a symptom of degradation. The survival of the fittest, which the West discovered recently and made into a law of life, our philosophers knew perfectly well — as the law of the jungle. We recognised desire, anger and the rest among the six lower tendencies of human nature — but we did not build civilised life on them. There are thieves and robbers in society; we protect ourselves from them; but we don't make them our ideals. Civilisations have developed not on the jungle law but by working out how its operation in human life can be minimised.

Because alongside conflict and competition, the world contains cooperation in abundance. Vegetation and animal life keep each other alive — plants give us oxygen, we give plants the carbon dioxide they need. Mutual cooperation sustains life on this earth. Recognising this mutual sustenance among forms of life, and making human life mutually sustaining — that, he says, is the prime characteristic of civilisation. To mould nature to achieve social goals is culture; when nature leads to social conflict, that is perversion. Culture does not deny nature — it enhances what sustains life and curbs what destroys it.

He grounds this in the most ordinary things. The relations of brother and sister, mother and son — these are natural, the same in humans and animals. Two brothers are sons of one mother; two calves are children of one cow. What's the difference? Animals, lacking memory, cannot build anything on these relations. Human beings take these natural relations and construct a harmonious order of life on them — new relationships flowing from the basic ones, knitting the whole society into a single unit of cooperation. Values and traditions are built this way; standards of good and bad are constructed accordingly. Society contains both affection between brothers and enmity between brothers — but we call affection good and try to enhance it. If instead you make conflict and enmity the basis of human relations, and analyse all of history on that basis, then dreaming of world peace out of such an analysis is futile. A mother brings up her children, and mother's love is held up as the highest love — on such a basis we can build the rules of mankind's life. Yes, nature also shows the opposite — there are species where the mother devours her own progeny; there are monkeys who carry a dead child long after it has died. Both exist in nature. Which of the two do you make the basis of civilised life? Only that which sustains life. Anger and greed are in our nature — but so are love and sacrifice. If we make anger the standard and arrange life accordingly, the result is disharmony. Hence the old exhortation: do not yield to anger. Control — not anger — becomes the standard of life.

And such laws, he says, are the principles of ethics — and here comes one of the loveliest points in the lecture: these principles are not framed by anyone. They are discovered. Just like the law of gravitation. Newton did not frame the law that a stone falls to the ground — he saw the apple fall, realised such a law must exist, and discovered it. In the same way, "control your anger", or "do not tell lies — say what you know to be true" — these are discovered laws of human relations, whose usefulness shows itself at every step of life. A child, by nature, does not lie. Often it is the parents who teach it — they hide a toy and tell the child it has disappeared; the child is fooled once or twice, then learns the trick, and learns to speak untruth. That a person is by nature truthful is itself a discovered law.

In Bharat, these discovered principles — the laws that bring harmony, peace and progress to human life — are given a name: Dharma. The laws of life. And Upadhyaya is emphatic about the translation: Dharma is rendered here as law. The English word religion is not the correct word for Dharma. When nature is channelled according to the principles of Dharma, we get culture and civilisation. On this foundation, he now turns to the individual.

Because the integrated view applies not just to collective life but to each person. Normally we think of an individual as a body — physical comfort and luxury equal happiness. But mental worry destroys bodily happiness. Everyone wants comfort — but put a man in prison and serve him the finest food: is he happy? A fine meal accompanied by a few insults gives no joy. He reaches for the Mahabharata: when Lord Krishna went to Hastinapur as the Pandavas' emissary, Duryodhana invited him to enjoy royal hospitality. Krishna declined — and went instead to Vidura's home. And Vidura's wife, overjoyed, beside herself at the arrival of this revered guest, served him the banana skins and threw away the fruit — and Krishna relished even the skins. Hence the saying: a modest meal served with dignity and affection tastes better than the finest delicacies served with disrespect. So mental happiness counts.

And beyond the mind, the intellect. A person may have bodily comfort and affection, but if he is caught in intellectual confusion, he is reduced to something like madness. What is a lunatic? He may have every physical comfort, perfect health, caring relatives — what he lacks is intellectual peace. That too is essential.

Body, mind, intelligence and the soul — these four together make up an individual. But — and this is the sentence at the heart of the whole philosophy — these are integrated. We cannot think of each part separately. The confusion in the West arose exactly from treating each aspect of the human being separately, without relation to the rest. He tells it as a story. When the movement for democracy came, they proclaimed man a political animal, and to satisfy this political man they gave him the vote — everyone shall rule! But then the question arose: the vote is nice, but what about food? What shall I do with the state if I have no bread? Then came Karl Marx and said: yes, bread is the most important thing; the state belongs to the haves; let us fight for bread. Marx saw man as primarily a body wanting bread. But those who followed Marx's path found, in the end, that they had neither bread nor the vote. And at the opposite end stands the U.S.A. — where there is both bread and the vote. And even so, no peace, no happiness. America, he notes, tops the lists in suicides, in mental patients, in people who need tranquilisers to sleep. Man got bread, man got the vote — and now he wants his peaceful sleep back, and sound sleep is a scarce commodity in present-day America. The thinking ones realise there is a basic mistake somewhere — that even after acquiring all the good things of life, they are not happy.

The reason, says Upadhyaya, is that they have not thought of the integrated human being. In our country we considered this thoroughly, and stated that the progress of man means progress of body, mind, intellect and soul — all together. He then clears up a misconception: it is often propagated that Bharatiya culture cares only for the salvation of the soul and bothers about nothing else. Wrong, he says. Others gave importance to the body alone — so our attention to the soul looked unique, and over time created the impression that we cared only for the soul. He gives a domestic analogy: an unmarried young man cares for his mother; after marriage he cares for his wife and his mother both, discharging his duty to each — would you say he no longer loves his mother? A wife loves her husband; after a child is born she loves husband and child both — the unthinking husband who feels neglected is generally wrong. Likewise: attending to the soul does not mean neglecting the body. The Upanishads declare in unambiguous words that a weakling cannot realise the self. The body is the primary instrument for discharging what Dharma enjoins. And here is the fundamental difference with the West: they regarded the body and the satisfaction of its desires as the aim; we regard the body as an instrument for achieving our aims. Satisfying bodily needs is necessary — it is just not the sole aim of all effort.

So what is the full picture of human effort? Bharat placed before itself the fourfold responsibility — catering to body, mind, intellect and soul — through four kinds of human effort: Dharma, Artha, Kama, and Moksha. The Purusharthas — efforts that befit a human being. The longing for all four is inborn. And crucially, these four were also thought of in an integrated way. Moksha — liberation — is the highest of them, yet effort aimed at Moksha alone is not considered the way to it; rather, the person who acts while remaining unattached to the fruits of action achieves Moksha inevitably, and earlier. Artha covers political and economic life — for the ancients it included justice and punishment. Kama is the satisfaction of natural desires. And Dharma is the set of rules that regulates Artha and Kama so that life progresses harmoniously and attains not only wealth and pleasure but eventually liberation.

Though Dharma regulates the other two, all three are interrelated and mutually complementary. Dharma helps achieve Artha: even in business you need honesty, restraint, truthfulness — without them you cannot earn. The Americans said, honesty is the best business policy. The Europeans said, honesty is the best policy. We go one step further and say: honesty is not a policy but a principle. We follow Dharma not because it pays, but because it is the fundamental principle of civilised life. Kama, too, is attained only through Dharma — having produced good things, when, where, how, and in what measure to enjoy them, only Dharma can decide. If a sick man eats the food meant for the healthy, and the healthy man the food meant for the sick, both suffer. Dharma restrains natural tendencies so that a man can tell what is beneficial from what is merely pleasurable.

But the dependence runs the other way too, and Upadhyaya will not let the ascetics off the hook: Dharma cannot be practised in the absence of Artha. What sin will not be committed by one who is starving? Those who have lost everything become ruthless. Even a rishi like Vishwamitra, driven by hunger, broke into a hunter's home and ate the flesh of a dog. Therefore, we are enjoined to create wealth continuously — wealth strengthens Dharma. The state must maintain law and order, because chaos destroys Dharma — in chaos, the jungle law prevails and the strong feed on the weak. Stability of the state is essential for Dharma to prevail. And yet — the opposite extreme is equally fatal: excessive power of the state is also harmful to Dharma. A king should be neither too harsh nor too soft. When the state usurps the rightful place of Dharma — when it acquires all powers, political and economic — Dharma declines. The whole society begins to look to the state for everything; officers neglect their duties and acquire vested interests. So Artha must not be allowed to dominate in either direction — neither absent, nor absolute.

Kama gets the same treatment. If physical needs are neglected and desires entirely suppressed, Dharma does not grow — Dharma cannot be observed on an empty stomach. If the fine arts that satisfy the mind are stopped altogether, the civilising influence disappears, the mind becomes perverse, and Dharma is neglected. But if the greed of the gluttons of Rome, or the sensuousness of Yayati, prevails — duties are forgotten. So Kama must be pursued consistent with Dharma.

And that completes the second evening's picture: the life of the individual considered thoroughly and integrally — body, mind, intellect and soul developed in balance, the manifold aspirations of man satisfied without one effort conflicting with another. This concept of the complete human being, the integrated individual, Upadhyaya says, is both our goal and our path.

He closes with the question for the next evening: what should be the relation of this integrated human being with society? That is episode three — society as a living organism, the soul of a nation called Chiti, and why Dharma, not the state, and not even the majority, is supreme. Thank you for listening.
