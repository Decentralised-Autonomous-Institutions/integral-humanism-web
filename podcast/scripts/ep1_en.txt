Welcome to Integral Humanism, a four-part audio series on one of the most original works of political philosophy to come out of independent India. Over four evenings in Bombay, from the twenty-second to the twenty-fifth of April, nineteen sixty-five, Pandit Deendayal Upadhyaya delivered four lectures. Together, they came to be known as Integral Humanism — Ekatma Manav-vad. This series walks through all four lectures, one episode per lecture, keeping every argument, every story, and every example intact — just told the way you'd tell it to a friend.

A quick word about the man himself. Deendayal Upadhyaya was born in nineteen sixteen in a small village near Mathura. A brilliant student — gold medals at matriculation, gold medals again at Intermediate, a first-class degree in mathematics. He joined the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh in nineteen thirty-seven, and in nineteen fifty-two became General Secretary of the newly formed Bharatiya Jana Sangh, a party he quietly built for fifteen years before becoming its President. He was an editor, a playwright, a biographer — and in February nineteen sixty-eight, he died in tragic circumstances, leaving behind this, his most complete statement of what he believed.

So. Episode one. The first lecture, delivered on the twenty-second of April, nineteen sixty-five. If you had to give it a title, it would be the question that haunts the whole evening: Whither Bharat? Where is India going?

Upadhyaya begins with a blunt observation. When the British ruled, every movement in the country had a single, simple aim — drive out the foreign rulers, achieve independence. But what would the new Bharat look like after independence? Which direction would it take? He's careful here: it isn't that nobody had thought about this. Gandhi had set out his vision in Hind Swaraj. Before him, Lokmanya Tilak had examined the philosophical basis of India's rejuvenation in Gita Rahasya, comparing schools of thought from all over the world. Congress and other parties passed resolutions. But the subject never got the serious study it deserved, because everyone believed the first task was getting the British out — the rest could be discussed later. Even those who wanted socialism as the basis of a future Bharat simply worked inside the Congress as a socialist group. The revolutionaries worked in their own way. Everyone agreed on one thing only: independence first.

And then comes the sting. Having attained independence, the question should naturally have arisen — now that we are free, what shall be the direction of our progress? And yet, Upadhyaya says, it is amazing that seventeen years after independence, we still cannot say that a definite direction has been decided.

Yes, slogans exist. Welfare State. Socialism. Liberalism. But he insists — and he says this from personal experience — the slogans carry little real philosophy. He tells a story. A leading political gentleman once suggested to him that all parties should form a joint front against the Congress, so that a good fight could be given. Upadhyaya asked the obvious question: what programme shall we adopt? If we form a front, we need some idea of a common programme — what will our economic policy be, what will our foreign policy be? And the gentleman replied, don't worry about it — whatever you like, you can adopt. We are ready to support anything, from extreme Marxist to downright capitalist programme. The reply came as if this was the most natural thing in the world. The only object was that Congress should somehow be defeated.

He points to Kerala, where elections had just been held. Communists, the Muslim League, the Swatantra Party, the S.S.P., the Kerala Congress, the Revolutionary Socialist Party — all entering a bewildering variety of alliances, until it was impossible to imagine that any of these parties had a definite ideology or principles at all.

And the Congress itself? It had proclaimed democratic socialism as its goal, but its leaders' behaviour showed there was no single direction inside it. There were staunch communists in the Congress fold, and there were people who had complete faith in capitalism and opposed communism to the teeth. And then comes one of his most famous lines: if there can be a magic box which contains a cobra and a mongoose living together — it is the Congress.

Can a country progress in this condition? Upadhyaya's diagnosis is that the confusion about our goal and direction is the main cause of the chaos. Now, he is realistic — four hundred and fifty million people cannot agree on everything, no country manages that. But every nation has something like a common desire, a more or less shared longing of its people. If that popular longing is made the basis of national aims, the common man feels the nation is moving in the right direction, that his own aspirations are reflected in the nation's efforts. And that produces the greatest possible feeling of unity.

He offers proof: the Chinese invasion of October and November nineteen sixty-two. A wave of enthusiasm swept the country. Action and sacrifice came in abundance. The barriers between government and public, between the political parties — they simply dissolved. Why? Because the external threat made us recognise ourselves. The government's policy reflected a widespread feeling in the people, and it enhanced their self-respect with a call for sacrifice. The result: we stood united.

That phrase — the threat made us recognise ourselves — leads to the heart of the lecture. The root of our problems, Upadhyaya says, is the neglect of the self. Of national identity. Without knowing our national identity, independence has no meaning, and it cannot become an instrument of progress and happiness. He draws an analogy from psychology: when a person's natural instincts are suppressed, mental disorders follow. The person becomes restless, dejected; his abilities deteriorate and become perverted. A nation is the same. When its natural instincts — its innate nature — are disregarded, it falls prey to numerous ills. The basic cause of the problems facing Bharat is the neglect of its national identity. You cannot go against nature; but you can elevate nature to the level of culture.

And because the people leading the nation are not aware of this root cause, he says, opportunists with no principles reign in our politics. Parties and politicians have neither principles, nor aims, nor a standard code of conduct. People leave one party and join another and feel nothing. Alliances, mergers, splits — dictated not by principle, but purely by gains in elections and positions of power. He remembers a healthier time. In nineteen thirty-nine, Hafiz Mohammed Ibrahim was elected on a Muslim League ticket. When he later joined the Congress, he did the honourable thing — he resigned his seat and sought re-election on a Congress ticket, and won again. In nineteen forty-eight, when the socialists left Congress and founded the Socialist Party, every one of their legislators resigned and fought fresh elections on their new tickets. But that healthy tradition was forgotten. Now, he says, there is complete licence in politics — and as a result, in the public mind there is distrust of everyone. Hardly a single person whose integrity is beyond doubt. And without trust, unity and discipline cannot come.

So the nation is at a crossroads. Which way? Some people say: let us go back to where we were a thousand years ago, when foreign invaders disrupted our life, and start again from there. Upadhyaya rejects this beautifully. A nation is not an inanimate object, not a piece of cloth where you can pick up the weaving after a gap. And it isn't even true that our national life stood still for a thousand years — the nation put her genius to work in changing circumstances, struggled, wrested independence back. The current of national life was never interrupted. And then the image that carries the whole argument: the Ganga at Banaras may not be crystal clear like it is at Haridwar. It has absorbed countless streams along the way, with all their refuse. But it is still the same holy Ganga — and the Ganga must flow onwards. You cannot turn her waters back to some earlier point.

But — and this matters — while we were absorbed in fighting invaders and fighting for independence, other nations made phenomenal progress. We could not contribute to the world's progress. Now that we are free, we must fill this deficiency and stand shoulder to shoulder with the advanced nations. Up to this point, he says, nobody disagrees.

The difficulty begins when we try to explain the West's spectacular advance — and what to copy from it. The British ruled us for a century, and during that time they subtly created in our minds a contempt for everything Bharatiya and a respect for everything Western. Along with science came their way of life, their manners, their food habits, their social, economic and political doctrines — all became our standards. We took pride in resisting things British while they ruled us. But strangely, once the British left, westernisation became synonymous with progress. Upadhyaya's distinction here is precise, and it's one of the most quoted ideas in the lecture: western science and the western way of life are two different things. Western science is universal — we must absorb it if we want to move forward. But the western way of life and values? Thoughtless imitation of the West must be scrupulously discarded.

So before deciding where to take the country, he examines the West's three great isms — nationalism, democracy, and socialism — asking where each came from, and what each actually delivered.

Nationalism first, the oldest and strongest. After the fall of the Roman Empire and the decline of the Catholic Church, Europe saw the rise of nations. The history of Europe for a thousand years is the history of the rise of, and conflict among, nations — nations that extended their empires beyond Europe and subjugated independent countries. Nationalism fused nation and state into nation-states, and the decline of the Church's influence gave rise to national churches, or to the disappearance of religion from politics — the origin of the secular state.

Then democracy. Every nation once had a king, but people gradually awakened against royal autocracy. The industrial revolution and international trade created a business class in every nation, and this new centre of power clashed with kings and feudal lords. That conflict adopted democracy as its philosophical basis, tracing its origins to the Greek city republics. The common man was drawn by the lofty ideals of equality, fraternity, and liberty. France had a bloody revolution; England had its movements; royalty was liquidated or curbed, constitutional governments established. And here Upadhyaya's irony: today, even those who suppress democracy do not denounce it — Hitler, Mussolini, Stalin, all paid lip service to democracy.

But — and this is the third movement — the individual got a vote, while real power stayed with those who led the revolution. The industrial revolution moved the worker from the freedom of his home into the factory, taking orders from owners, migrating to crowded cities with no proper housing, no protective rules. Economically weak, not yet organised, he became a victim of exploitation, injustice, and harassment. And those who held political power belonged to the same class that was exploiting him — so there was no hope of redress from the state.

Out of that injustice came the socialists. Karl Marx was one of them — he studied history and the structure of society, presented his analysis, and claimed a scientific basis for it. Later socialists didn't all agree with Marx, but all were influenced by him. Marx's analysis — dialectical materialism — located the root of exploitation in private ownership of the means of production. Make those means the property of society — and for the Marxist, society means the state — and exploitation ends. But first, the state must be wrested from the exploiters, and protected from their influence — hence the dictatorship of the proletariat. And to make people tolerate this dictatorship, an ideal was held out: once the exploiter class is finally liquidated, the state will wither away into a classless, stateless society. Marx also claimed capitalism carries the seeds of its own destruction, making socialism inevitable.

Some countries had socialist revolutions. Even where socialism was not accepted, politicians had to accept workers' rights, and the Welfare State became an ideal.

So there stand the three doctrines that have dominated European thinking — nationalism, democracy, and socialism, with equality at socialism's root. Plus, now and then, the ideals of world peace and world unity. All good ideas, Upadhyaya grants — they reflect the higher aspirations of mankind. But — and this is the lecture's crucial finding — each of these doctrines is incomplete by itself, and in practice, each stands opposed to the others. Nationalism poses a threat to world peace. Democracy and capitalism join hands to give free rein to exploitation. Socialism replaced capitalism — and brought with it the end of democracy and individual freedom.

The West has been trying to reconcile these good ideals, and has not succeeded to this day. England emphasised nationalism and democracy and built her institutions along those lines. France couldn't manage the same — democracy there produced political instability. The British Labour Party wanted to reconcile socialism with democracy, and people doubted democracy would survive if socialism gained strength — so Labour diluted its socialism considerably. And Hitler and Mussolini took nationalism plus socialism, and buried democracy — with socialism becoming a mere tool of a nationalism that threatened world peace itself.

So the conclusion of the first evening. We may seek some guidance from the western world — but the fact is, it has no concrete suggestions to offer. The West is itself at a crossroads, unable to decide what is good. We cannot expect guidance from it. On the contrary, we must ask whether we can contribute something to resolve the world's dilemma. As a member of the world community, we must discharge our responsibilities. In this era of adulteration, he says with a smile, instead of adulterating ideas, let us scrutinise and improve them before accepting them. Rather than being a burden on the world, let us try to solve the world's problems.

And he closes with the promise that sets up the next lecture: we must also consider what contribution our own tradition and civilisation can make to world culture. We shall consider this tomorrow evening.

That is where we'll pick up in episode two: the integral view of life — what Bharatiya culture actually says about the human being, body, mind, intellect and soul, and the four aims of human effort. Thank you for listening.
